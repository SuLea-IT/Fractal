MATERIALS AND METHODS
Plant materials
The following A. thaliana lines in the Columbia-0 background were used: lfy-12 (40); ap1-7 (6),
cal-1 (41), ap1-7 cal-1 double mutants (gift from J. Goodrich, Institute of Molecular Plant Science,
University of Edinburgh, Edinburgh, United Kingdom), SOC1p-GFP (42) (provided by D. Posé),
35Sp:FT (43), 35Sp:SOC1 (44), soc1-2 (44), AGL24p:AGL24-RFP (45) and agl24-2 (28).
35Sp:CO (46), co-3 (47), ft-3 (43), 35S:AP1-GR ap1-1 cal-1 (48) and TFL1p:GUS (this work) are
A. thaliana lines in the Landsberg erecta background, as well as the 35S:AP1-GR ap1-1 cal-1 (48)
used for the mutation of CLV3 using the CRISPR-Cas9 strategy.
For crosses between accessions, it was previously verified that the GUS reporter activity was not
affected in wild-type Col x Ler hybrids.
Brassica oleracea var. botrytis and Romanesco cauliflowers were obtained from Observatoire
Breton des Semences (OBS) or bought at the Grenoble producer market.
Plant growth conditions
Seeds were sown on soil or surface-sterilized and grown in Petri dishes on Murashige and Skoog
basal salt mixture medium (Sigma-Aldrich, www.sigmaaldrich.com). Seeds were stratified 1-3
days at 4°C, transferred mixture of phagnum:perlite:vermiculite (2:1:1) or on soil:vermiculite (5:1)
and grown at 21-22°C under long-day (16 h) or short-day conditions (8 h). ap1 cal plants were
grown at 18°C for 16 h light and 16°C for 8 h dark, under 65% humidity. Nicotiana benthamiana
plants for transient assays were grown on a mixture of sphagnum:vermiculite (1:1) under long-day
photoperiod at 24°C.

GUS assay and histological procedures
Arabidopsis shoot apices were stained for GUS as described (20) with minor modifications.
Staining buffer contained 10 mM of potassium ferri- and ferrocyanide in order to minimize leakage

Images of cleared apices were taken with a Nikon DS-Fi1 digital camera connected to a Nikon

were stained in 0.2% (w/v) eosin and embedded in paraffin as described (20). 12 µm sections were
obtained with a Leica RM-2025 microtome and images were taken under bright-field microscopy.

MODELING METHODS
Regulatory network construction and simulation
SALT network
The SALT model included the SAX genes (i.e., SUPPRESSOR OF OVEREXPRESSION OF
CONSTANS 1 (SOC1), AGAMOUS-LIKE 24 (AGL24) and XAANTAL2 (XAL2)), APETALA1
(AP1) and CAULIFLOWER (CAL) (hereinafter AP1/CAL), LEAFY (LFY), and TERMINAL
FLOWER1 (TFL1). These genes are the key transcription factors (TF) or co-factors that have been
experimentally associated with inflorescence and flower development and the development of
cauliflower structures in A. thaliana. We also included two inputs, auxin, a phytohormone that
promotes the initiation of new primordia, and F that represents different types of molecules
triggering flower production. Because we did not include the auxin signaling pathway and F
possibly represents multiple flowering inducing pathways, the activity of both auxin and F is only
phenomenological in our model. Auxin concentration is maximal in newly formed primordia,
while its activity is basal in the shoot apical meristem. For this reason, shoot apical meristems and
newly formed primordia states are different in their auxin levels. As the value of F increases during
the plant development in every meristem, we expect the shoot apical meristem to transit to an
inflorescence state, and to a flower state for lateral primordia. The network includes multiple
positive and negative regulatory interactions listed in Table S1. We model all regulatory
interaction using Hill equation of the form:

Parameters
Data for the exact value of the parameters included in the network are not available in the literature.
However, we used general knowledge about the different processes to constrain the parameter
space. In particular, we assumed that the degradation 𝛿 of all variables were equal to 0.1/minute,
which is a realistic value given that the half-life of proteins is usually within the hours range (68).
The level of cooperativity 𝑛 for all X and Y genes were equal to 2, representing the formation of
9

===== PAGE 11 =====

protein dimers for gene regulations, which is common for gene regulation (69). Finally, we
considered that the concentration of TFs in cells is within the nanoMolar (nM) range (70). The
affinity of transcription factor to the DNA is usually lower than 10−8M (i.e., affinity≤µM)(71).
Finally, TFs or cofactors, including TFL1, AP1, and LFY bind to hundreds of target genes, and all
TFs can randomly bind to the DNA (32). Thus, the effective concentration of TFs is lower than
the real concentration. Assuming that the effective concentrations of the TFs is 2 orders of
magnitude smaller than the real concentration, we constrain 𝑘 values within the range
𝑋,𝑌
[10−10M, 10−6M].

Structure of the model
The model structure (as reported in Fig. 3a, with feedback loops and feedforward loops (FFL)
listed in Table S3), allows for some analysis which is independent of specific parameter values.
The “mutual repression” positive feedback listed in Table S3 is solely mediated by AP1. The other
positive 2-loop, between AP1 and LFY, is visible but cannot affect the competition with TFL1: it
plays a role of stabilization, or self-maintenance, of the floral identity. One consequence of this
loop is that, at steady state, LFY and AP1 are always expected to be either both high or low. It is
apparent, without having to write any detailed model of the dynamics, that the removal of AP1
above deprives the network from its ability to overcome TFL1's expression; even if external signals
transiently increase LFY's expression, without AP1’s contribution this cannot be maintained
permanently and the system must reverse to a ``non-flower'' identity (high TFL1). The structure of
the positive three loop entails that any steady state must not only have AP1 and LFY at similar
levels, but that this should be opposite to TFL1 level, again confirming the antagonism floral/non-
floral identities.
Coherent FFLs have been characterized (72) to induce a response delay (to “off steps” for type 2
and “on steps” for type 4, given the AND nature of these FFLs), which can have the function of a
10

===== PAGE 12 =====

“persistence detector” (i.e. their last term responds to pulses of the first term only if it persists over
time), while incoherent FFLs have the potential to speed up response times (the “on step” for type
1 and “off step” for type 3, with also a potential to induce pulses of TFL1 if LFY is present). For
the SALT model this leads to the following interpretation: the GRN comprises a core “ALT”
network, which has an intrinsic ability to present clearly distinct floral (high LFY and AP1, low
TFL1) and non-floral attractors (high TFL1, low AP1 and LFY), while also presenting homeostatic
properties able to transiently maintain the network in intermediary states (all three core variables
at intermediate levels). On top of this core network, a layer of feedforward loops is able to speed
up the triggering of the core ALT network’s internal dynamics, while protecting the flower
inducing genes from transient removal of the florigen signal. Without the AP1 node, the remaining
LFY-TFL1 “core” network is irremediably deprived of its bistability, and only high TFL1/low
LFY can be achieved as a permanent state.
Two remaining FFLs (6th and 11th in Table S3) have the potential to make the increase (in
response to SAX, i.e. F after a delay) of LFY faster than that of TFL1 and make the removal of
TFL1 (upon SAX removal) delayed compared to that of LFY. Both can contribute to the
occurrence of a transient pulse of LFY upon F induction via SAX, during which TFL1 remains
low.

Coupling of plant architecture model and GRN
To model the interaction between the GRN studied in this work and the development of plant
architecture, we built a model for organ and branching system development and coupled this
developmental model with the GRN model presented in the previous section.
Our developmental model consists of a set of developmental rules expressing the growth of the
plant apices and the growth dynamics of the different organs. These rules are encoded using the
L-system formalism (73) in the L-Py programming language (74). The model is a context-free
deterministic L-system (D0L-system), where components change according to physiological and
genetic rules depending on their own state, but independent of the state of their neighbors in the
plant.

Meristem state. We assume in our model that plant apices i (i.e. meristems) have a state 𝑠
𝑖
composed of molecular variables (levels of gene expression, hormones, etc.), geometric variables
(radius, order), and physiological variables such as age, time count since last production,
dormancy, physiological age, number of plastochrons already produced, duration in the current
physiological state.
At a macroscopic level, meristems can be in one of three categories of physiological state
corresponding to three broad classes of the detailed states 𝑠 : VEG for vegetative, INFLO for
𝑖
inflorescence, FLO for flower. The first meristem starts in a VEG physiological state. During
growth, new meristems are initialized with respect to the parent meristem state 𝑠 : they inherit all
𝑖
the values LFY, AP1, SAX of their parent meristem, a value of TFL1 multiplied by a fraction
factor (by default 0.1) and maximal level of auxin and of TFL1 repressor (eRep). The value of F
is also inherited with potentially an attenuating factor (by default 1 - no attenuation).
Rules determining meristem macroscopic physiological state. At any time t, meristems that
have a state with high LFY and high AP1 are set to FLO physiological state. Meristems that are
not in FLO physiological state can either be in VEG or INFLO physiological states. A VEG
physiological state corresponds to a low sum of SAX and LFY, while INFLO physiological state
corresponds to a high value of the sum of SAX and LFY. The fact that the sum of LFY and SAX
determine the physiological state reflects the fact that LFY and SAX induce bolting independently.
Low/high values of specific gene activities are determined with respect to specific thresholds
parameters. These thresholds were set so that in WT plants they lead to realistic numbers of organs
and development (inspired from Mündermann et al. (75)).
Meristem growth. In VEG physiological state, meristems produce a short internode, an axillary
leaf at a phyllotaxis angle of 137 degrees from the preceding one at each plastochron. Axillary
meristems are forced to stay dormant (we do not model the outgrowth and dynamics of lateral
meristems in the rosette). In INFLO physiological state, a bract/cauline leaf is produced if the X
factor (reflecting a memory of high levels of LEAFY) is not set in the current meristem state,
together with an axillary meristem. Finally, as soon as a meristem enters a FLO state, it
immediately transforms into a terminal flower with age 0.
Conditions for lateral productions to grow out: The meristem building the main stem (order 0
in the code) grows immediately after initiation. Lateral meristems in the rosette meristem never

grow (their growth is not modelled). Meristems building stems of order 1 (lateral branches) always
start to grow with a small and constant delay with respect to the moment of their initiation (half a
day by default). Other meristems (at higher orders) are latent by default (in reference to what is
observed in WT, presumably due to apical dominance exerted to apices of order 1 on meristems
of higher orders). This dormancy is cancelled if meristems have experienced a high level of LFY
since their initiation, which by triggering the factor X (see above), induces meristems outgrowth
(case of cauliflower).
Organ growth. Every generated organ grows during the simulation according to its own growth
rules. These rules depend on the physiological state of the meristem that produced that organ. For
instance, an internode produced by a meristem in VEG physiological state will not elongate, as
opposed to meristems produced by meristems in INFLO or FLO physiological states. Growth rules
are essential to the dynamics of architecture development, in particular the rules controlling
internode elongation. These rules have been implemented to reproduce qualitatively realistic
rosette dynamics, bolting and flowering development on the WT. We assume here that these rules
are not modified by the mutations affecting the inflorescence studied here. The cauliflower
architecture thus emerges only from a change in the genetic network and its consequence on organ
outgrowth or suppression, with a conserved dynamics of axes elongation.
Gene regulation computation. At each time step, each meristem reads the new value of F, whose
dynamics is considered as a global input variable to the plant and determined here by a growing
sigmoidal function of the current development time. This value is used as an input of the genetic
network, together with the previous state of the meristem to update the genetic state of the
meristem. This new genetic state may in turn determine a new value of the macroscopic
physiological state of the meristem and induce a change in meristem behavior according to
previously defined rules.
A consequence of the meristem development rules and the monotonic increase of F, all meristems
follow a logical sequence of differentiation by moving to the next physiological state after a while:
VEG → INFLO → FLO,
thus producing a progressive macroscopic differentiation of the meristems. In the cauliflower, this
differentiation is blocked in the INFLO state as LFY can never set up firmly the flower state

Geometric model
In addition to the GRN-based plant development model, we built a purely architectural or
geometric model (no GRN involved) to study how changes in the plastochron modify the
morphology of curds. This model is a simplified version of the plant architecture model described
above.
In this model, a meristem (represented by a sphere of constant size) produces an elongating
internode and a lateral meristem at each plastochron. A newly born meristem starts producing its
own lateral meristems after a time defined by the parameter called Onset Delay (OD). Increasing
OD values decreases the total number of meristems produced by the simulation. OD = 0 was used
in Figure 4F-G. Internodes elongate in time according to a common sigmoidal curve. Starting from
an initial meristem, the repetitive application of these rules generates a curd composed of
branching systems called florets (fig. S6A). To avoid the high computational cost after some time
due to the production of an exponentially increasing number of elements, we simulated meristems
and internodes up to a chosen maximal order (MO). Florets of order = MO were represented by an
axis bearing a sphere used as envelope to coarsely approximate the non-simulated internodes and
meristems of higher order (fig. S6A). However, for long simulation times, the growth curve of the
envelopes creates large structures that become poor approximations of the oldest florets and alter
the actual curd structure (fig. S6A and orange spheres in fig. S6B). For a given number of
simulation days, more precise curd structures are obtained by increasing the detail level (i.e. by
increasing the MO value): fig. S6B shows that the envelopes become a better approximation as
they are used for younger florets when MO is increased. To limit the visual impact of poorly
simulated floret envelopes at low MO, we thus designed an empirical rule that removes those large
elements artificially altering the curd shape. This produces a curd structure closer to the fully
detailed one (i.e. with all orders simulated, Fig S6C). For example, removing the oldest envelopes
from the MO=4 model makes it closer to the finer one obtained at MO=9 (fig. S6B).
Limiting simulation time or increasing the OD parameter make it possible to reduce the number
of simulated meristems. In such cases, all meristems can be simulated at all orders and one can
optionally choose to inactivate the previous empirical rule and represent all meristems by spheres
of constant size (fig. S6D-E).
